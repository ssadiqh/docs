# ANZCA Principal Engineer Interview Story Bank — Paragraph Format

Each item is presented as a compact interview-preparation paragraph while retaining its original STAR or approach structure.

## 1. Secure-by-Design
**Interview Theme or Variation Note:** Security leadership; secure-by-design; Zero Trust; identity and access management; risk management; non-functional requirements; avoiding late rework. **Also use for:** "How do you handle NFRs?"; "How do you work with security teams?"; "What does good architecture governance look like?" **Approach:** I build security and risk into a solution from day one rather than treating security as a final approval gate. I begin by identifying and mapping every entry and exit point in the solution. These may include: APIs Batch interfaces Events Downstream integrations External partner interfaces Administrative and operational interfaces For API-based integrations, I work with the API and identity teams to define the appropriate OAuth 2.0 and OIDC approach, including authentication, scopes and authorisation requirements. I work with the authorisation and access-control teams to apply least privilege and Zero Trust principles. I also work with engineering teams to ensure that the organisation's standards for audit logging, traceability and monitoring are incorporated into the implementation. Authentication, authorisation and auditability are first-class design concerns. They are considered alongside the functional design rather than being added after the solution has already been built. I engage security architects, risk stakeholders, identity specialists and relevant operational teams early in the design process. I also use peer reviews to expose risks and assumptions before they become expensive delivery issues. **Lesson:** Security becomes an enabler when it is designed into the solution from the beginning. Early engagement and clearly defined controls reduce late surprises, approval delays and avoidable rework.

## 2. Optum Healthcare API Framework — Failure and Learning
**Interview Theme or Variation Note:** Mistake or failure; something that did not go to plan; biggest professional lesson; stakeholder engagement; requirements gathering; solution adoption; leadership maturity. **Also use for:** "Tell me about a time stakeholder management went wrong"; "What would you do differently?"; "How has your approach to architecture evolved?"; "How do you validate requirements?" **Situation:** Early in my time at Optum, I led the design of a reusable healthcare API framework. Its purpose was to allow multiple provider applications to expose APIs that could then be consumed by partners and third-party application teams. The initiative was intended to create a common platform capability that could be reused across several application teams. **Task:** My responsibility was to define the architecture and deliver a technically reusable framework that application teams could adopt to expose their services consistently. **Action:** I focused heavily on the technical design and selected OSGi as a major part of the solution. The OSGi-based technical direction proved too complex and did not work as expected. More importantly, I had not completed sufficient requirements and solution analysis before committing to the design. I did not identify and engage all the consuming application teams early enough. As a result, the framework was designed largely from a central architecture perspective rather than being validated with the teams expected to use it. Although the framework was built, there was no strong adoption plan, shared ownership or commitment from the consuming teams. **Result:** The framework did not achieve the adoption that had been expected, and the initiative ultimately changed direction after significant investment. This was a difficult and humbling outcome. A substantial amount of the framework had been developed, but the technical approach was problematic and the initiative had not been designed with enough attention to adoption, stakeholder ownership and practical use by the consuming teams. **Lesson:** The experience changed how I approach architecture. Since then, **Requirements and Solution Analysis — RSA — is one of the first activities I perform**. I begin by: Understanding the problem before selecting technology Mapping all stakeholders and potential consumers Validating functional and non-functional requirements Testing assumptions with the teams that will build, operate and consume the solution Treating ownership and adoption as design requirements Using proofs of concept to validate both technical feasibility and stakeholder acceptance The key lesson was that successful architecture is not only about choosing and building the right technology. It also depends on people, ownership, adoption and involving the right stakeholders from the beginning.

## 3. NAB ECOM — Managing Competing Priorities Across Six Subsidiaries
**Interview Theme or Variation Note:** Competing priorities; tight deadlines; prioritisation; cross-stream coordination; dependency management; leadership without becoming a bottleneck; working under delivery pressure. **Also use for:** "How do you manage your time across multiple streams?"; "Tell me about a time you had too much on your plate"; "How do you avoid becoming a bottleneck?" **Situation:** In the NAB ECOM program, I was leading solution architecture for migrating customer data from six subsidiaries into a single enterprise Customer Master. The subsidiaries had different migration sequencing, data structures, cutover plans and levels of readiness. At the same time, front-book onboarding and servicing work was continuing, so multiple architecture and delivery streams were active in parallel. One subsidiary, Medfin, started later than the others and was struggling with data mapping and readiness. Its delay created a risk that it would fall behind the overall migration sequence. **Task:** I needed to determine where to focus my limited architecture time. I could not give all six streams the same level of attention. My responsibility was to help Medfin catch up without allowing the other streams to become dependent on me or slowing the wider program. **Action:** I deliberately prioritised Medfin because it represented the greatest immediate risk to the overall sequence. I worked closely with the Medfin business and engineering teams to understand and resolve the data-mapping issues and associated dependencies. Where business interpretation and mapping decisions were slowing progress, I brought in the Product Owner to validate business rules and accelerate decisions. This allowed me to focus on architecture, domain dependencies and the integration implications. For the other subsidiaries that were already progressing well, I relied on established architecture patterns, reusable mapping approaches and design guardrails. This allowed those streams to continue independently without requiring the same level of direct involvement from me. I regularly reassessed progress and focused effort on the issues that had the greatest impact across the program rather than dividing my time equally. **Result:** Medfin caught up sufficiently for all six migration streams to progress in parallel without becoming a delay to the wider program. The architecture function did not become a bottleneck, and the teams were able to continue using agreed patterns and guardrails while I concentrated on the highest-risk stream. **Lesson:** Managing competing priorities is not about distributing time equally. It is about understanding dependencies, identifying where delay creates the greatest program risk, concentrating effort there, and bringing in the right expertise at the right time. Clear guardrails also allow lower-risk work to continue without constant architecture intervention.

## 4. NAB ECOM — Challenging the Existing Service-to-Service Authorisation Model
**Interview Theme or Variation Note:** Challenging an existing decision or way of working; influencing security direction; secure-by-design; Zero Trust; balancing strategic architecture with delivery reality; risk escalation. **Also use for:** "Tell me about a time you pushed back on a technical decision"; "How do you handle a situation where the right answer isn't available in time?"; "Tell me about a time you influenced without authority" **Situation:** During the NAB ECOM program, I was the Platform Solution Designer for the Customer Master ecosystem. I identified a security gap in the existing API model. User-facing APIs had both authentication and authorisation because they operated with user context and scopes. However, for service-to-service communication, the enterprise authorisation platform did not support the required pattern. As a result, service-to-service calls relied mainly on authentication without an equivalent authorisation layer. **Task:** I needed to ensure that the Customer Master solution aligned with secure-by-design and Zero Trust principles while still meeting the program's delivery timeline. I also needed to challenge the existing enterprise model constructively rather than simply accepting that service authentication alone was sufficient. **Action:** I engaged the API security and authorisation teams and explained the technical concern and the risk associated with authenticated services having insufficiently controlled access. I presented the case for service-to-service authorisation and escalated the issue through relevant security leadership when it became clear that the enterprise platform could not provide native support within the project timeline. The broader security teams agreed with the direction in principle, but the platform capability could not be delivered in time for ECOM. Rather than allowing the ideal future-state dependency to block delivery, I worked with the Customer Master engineering team to implement an application-level service-to-service authorisation layer. This provided a practical control for the immediate solution while the longer-term enterprise direction continued to be considered. **Result:** The Customer Master solution was delivered with the necessary service-to-service security controls. The immediate project achieved a secure and practical outcome, and security leadership also agreed in principle that native service-to-service authorisation should become part of the longer-term enterprise capability. **Lesson:** Architecture often requires balancing strategic direction with delivery reality. A Principal Engineer should challenge the status quo where there is a genuine risk, but should also find a practical and secure way to deliver today when the enterprise platform cannot change immediately.

## 5. NAB ECOM — Throttling Migration to Protect a Legacy Downstream System
**Interview Theme or Variation Note:** Technical trade-off; major risk management; judgement under pressure; resilience; protecting data integrity; balancing speed and stability. **Also use for:** "Tell me about a difficult technical decision"; "How do you handle situations where speed conflicts with stability?"; "Tell me about a time you chose the slower but safer path" **Situation:** During a NAB customer migration, the migration platform was capable of ingesting approximately 100,000 customer profiles within an hour. One legacy downstream application could process only around 5,000 to 10,000 customer updates in the same period. This created a significant mismatch between the migration throughput and the downstream system's processing capacity. **Task:** I needed to determine how to continue the migration without overwhelming the legacy application, losing data consistency or destabilising downstream processing. The trade-off was between achieving the fastest possible migration and maintaining system stability and data integrity. **Action:** I assessed the available options and recommended throttling the migration flow to a rate that the downstream platform could safely process. This meant accepting a slower migration rate rather than allowing the faster upstream system to overwhelm the legacy application. The decision prioritised controlled processing, recoverability and integrity over raw speed. **Result:** The migration proceeded without destabilising the downstream application. The controlled throughput protected data integrity, reduced the likelihood of production incidents and allowed the migration to continue within an understood and manageable risk profile. **Lesson:** The fastest technical option is not always the best delivery decision. Sometimes the correct architectural trade-off is controlled progress rather than maximum speed, particularly when reliability, downstream capacity and data integrity are at risk.

## 6. NAB ECOM — Enterprise Customer Master Consolidation
**Interview Theme or Variation Note:** Complex enterprise solution; large-scale transformation; stakeholder alignment; customer master and data migration; regulated environment; transition architecture; architecture governance. **Also use for:** "Tell me about the most complex programme you've worked on"; "How do you manage stakeholder alignment across a large transformation?"; "Tell me about a time you delivered in a regulated environment" **Situation:** NAB had a fragmented customer identity landscape across six subsidiaries, including UBank, JBWere, NAB Trade, Medfin, Corporate and Institutional Banking and Corporate Cards. This fragmentation created compliance risk, inconsistent customer information and no single trusted enterprise customer view. The ECOM program was established to consolidate approximately two million customer records into enterprise Customer Master and Customer Establishment platforms while supporting compliant onboarding, servicing and downstream synchronisation. **Task:** As Platform Solution Designer, I needed to provide the architecture for the consolidation and align multiple business, engineering, security, risk, financial crime and downstream-system stakeholders. The solution had to support migration, coexistence and live operations without compromising security, regulatory compliance or production stability. **Action:** I developed transition-state architectures, migration sequencing, coexistence models, cutover strategies, rollback approaches and reconciliation frameworks. I designed canonical mapping, deduplication and survivorship approaches for the customer records and supported batch-processing patterns for validation, exception management, controlled restart and recovery. I also defined event-driven integration and orchestration patterns to keep the Customer Master and downstream systems synchronised during migration. Across the program, I facilitated collaborative workshops, made architecture trade-offs explicit, managed RAID items and worked with security, risk, product, engineering, enterprise architecture and financial crime stakeholders. I used governance artefacts and architecture reviews to build shared understanding and obtain the necessary approvals. **Result:** The program delivered a production-ready approach for consolidating approximately two million customer records across six subsidiaries. The solution achieved SIT sign-off, Zero Trust security approval and financial-crime risk clearance for live migration. The program also established a stronger foundation for enterprise customer onboarding, servicing and trusted customer data. **Lesson:** Large transformations depend as much on alignment as on technical design. Stakeholders build confidence when the architecture makes dependencies, trade-offs, risks and ownership clear, and when they are involved in shaping the solution rather than being asked only to approve it at the end.

## 7. NAB ECOM — JBWere Branded Credit Decline Letter
**Interview Theme or Variation Note:** Influencing without authority; CIO or executive advisory; architecture governance; risk-based decision-making; handling ambiguity; challenging a requirement or design; cross-domain coordination; knowing when to escalate. **Also use for:** "Tell me about a time you dealt with a difficult stakeholder"; "How do you handle ambiguity?"; "Tell me about a time there was no clean answer"; "How do you know when to escalate?" **Situation:** As part of the NAB customer-onboarding process, when a customer failed a credit check, a customer letter—referred to in the program as a CHL letter—had to be sent. For JBWere customers, the letter needed to carry JBWere branding. The challenge was that in the banker-assisted journey, product or brand selection occurred later in the onboarding flow. At the point where the credit check failed and the letter had to be triggered, there was no reliable brand context available. **Task:** I needed to identify a workable solution without assigning the capability or brand data to an application domain that did not legitimately own it. The solution needed to meet the immediate regulatory and business requirement while avoiding unnecessary technical debt and preserving clear domain boundaries. **Action:** I worked with business stakeholders, Product Owners, business architects and domain architects across Customer Master, Customer Establishment, Identity Verification and related systems. We assessed whether any of those systems could legitimately own the required brand context or letter capability. The analysis showed that no domain had clean ownership. Each simple option would introduce technical debt or place responsibility in a system that did not own the underlying business concept. Rather than forcing a poor-fit solution, I prepared an options paper that described the available approaches, the risks and the trade-offs. I escalated the matter to the appropriate executive forum so that the decision could be made consciously with the implications clearly understood. I recommended using the banker's Active Directory group information as an interim signal for brand context. I explicitly identified the limitation that a banker could potentially operate across both NAB and JBWere contexts, so the workaround was treated as a documented interim risk rather than the target state. I also recommended a target-state direction in which the letter-sending capability would be decoupled from Identity Verification and placed into a more appropriate independent capability. **Result:** The recommendation was accepted. The program adopted a pragmatic, risk-aware interim solution using banker information, with the limitations understood and documented. It also established a clear target-state direction for decoupling the letter capability rather than forcing a poor-fit solution into the wrong application domain. **Lesson:** Influence does not always come from formal authority. It comes from making the problem, options, risks and trade-offs clear enough for stakeholders to make a conscious decision. Good architecture governance protects domain boundaries while still allowing the organisation to move forward pragmatically.

## 8. NAB Credit Risk Engine — Limited Resources and Legacy Modernisation
**Interview Theme or Variation Note:** Working with limited resources; inexperienced or junior team; legacy modernisation; prove-then-scale; securing investment; mentoring; pragmatic engineering leadership. **Also use for:** "How do you approach legacy modernisation?"; "Tell me about a time you mentored junior engineers"; "How do you build a case for investment?"; "Tell me about a time you delivered with constraints" **Situation:** NAB's Credit Risk Engine was a large legacy monolithic application with long processing cycles and significant operational complexity. The modernisation opportunity was substantial, but the initiative initially had limited funding certainty and a constrained team composed largely of interns and junior engineers. **Task:** I needed to demonstrate that the monolith could be modernised successfully and that the proposed engineering approach was viable before the organisation committed to a larger investment. I also needed to guide a relatively inexperienced team toward building something that demonstrated production-scale potential rather than only a disposable prototype. **Action:** I began by understanding the existing application, its main pain points and the areas that would provide the clearest evidence of value. Rather than trying to modernise the entire platform at once, I selected a contained, high-impact capability that could be used to prove the architecture and engineering approach. I mentored the junior engineers, established design direction and engineering guardrails, and guided the team in building a proof of concept using the proposed Java and microservices approach. The proof of concept was used to validate technical feasibility, demonstrate the direction to stakeholders and support the case for further investment. **Result:** The proof of concept demonstrated that the modernisation approach was viable and helped secure support and funding to scale the initiative. The broader modernisation subsequently decomposed the legacy platform into Java and Spring Boot microservices and delivered major performance improvements, including reducing processing from 54 hours to 2 hours while maintaining regulatory compliance and production stability. **Lesson:** In a resource-constrained environment, the most effective approach is often to prove value in a focused area before scaling. A small team can achieve significant outcomes when the scope is carefully selected, architecture guardrails are clear and engineers receive the right mentoring and support.

## 9. Reviewing Vendor Designs
**Interview Theme or Variation Note:** Vendor assurance; architecture governance; constructive challenge; solution quality; commercial and risk awareness; stakeholder engagement; fit-for-purpose design. **Also use for:** "How do you assure technical quality when you don't own the design?"; "How do you challenge a vendor without being obstructive?"; "How do you balance governance with delivery pace?" **Approach:** I begin by establishing a clear baseline for assessment. That baseline includes: Business outcomes Functional requirements Non-functional requirements Regulatory obligations Security and privacy requirements Enterprise architecture principles API and integration standards Operational and support expectations I then review the vendor's proposed solution against that baseline. The review covers areas such as: End-to-end integration Data flows and data ownership APIs and interface specifications Sequence and interaction flows Identity, authentication and authorisation Security controls Observability and auditability Resilience and failure handling Deployment and operational support model Maintainability and lifecycle ownership Where I identify a gap, I challenge the design with evidence rather than opinion. I explain: Which requirement or principle is not being met The risk or consequence created by the gap The practical options available The trade-offs associated with those options My recommendation, where I have one Where the vendor has deeper product knowledge, I ask them to return with clear options and trade-offs rather than accepting a vague assurance that the product will handle the requirement. The conversation remains collaborative and focused on outcomes. The purpose is not to reject the vendor's proposal for architectural purity, but to ensure the solution is secure, supportable, maintainable and proportionate to the organisation's scale. **Lesson:** Effective vendor governance should improve the design rather than create an adversarial relationship. A Principal Engineer should challenge constructively, make risks visible and maintain clear accountability while avoiding unnecessary over-engineering.

## 10. Design Reviews and Engineering Governance
**Interview Theme or Variation Note:** Engineering governance; technical leadership; design assurance; collaborative reviews; early risk detection; avoiding bureaucracy; enabling delivery. **Also use for:** "How do you uplift engineering quality without slowing delivery?"; "How do you introduce standards to a team?"; "How do you run design reviews?"; "How do you balance rigour with speed?" **Approach:** In my current role, after completing the initial design walkthrough with the engineering team, I identify the areas that need deeper technical design. I also ask the engineering team to identify any additional areas where they believe greater clarity or design attention is required. These areas may include: Complex integration points Service boundaries Data ownership Security controls Failure handling Performance Deployment concerns Operational supportability I then establish short, focused design-review sessions early in the delivery lifecycle. The reviews are not intended to be large governance gates. They are collaborative working sessions where engineers can walk through their detailed design, surface uncertainties and validate implementation decisions before too much work is committed. Where appropriate, these sessions may occur once or twice a week during the early stages of implementation, with the frequency reducing as the design stabilises. The objective is to provide fast feedback, maintain alignment to the architecture and prevent teams from drifting too far before an issue is detected. **Lesson:** Good engineering governance is lightweight, early and collaborative. It should help teams make better decisions and maintain momentum, rather than turning architecture into a documentation or approval bottleneck.

## 11. NAB Onboarding — AI-Enabled AML Document Intelligence
**Interview Theme or Variation Note:** Innovation in a regulated environment; AI adoption; compliance; human-in-the-loop controls; balancing automation and risk; modern engineering approaches. **Also use for:** "How do you approach AI in a regulated environment?"; "Tell me about a time you introduced a new technology with compliance constraints"; "How do you balance innovation with risk?" **Situation:** Within NAB customer onboarding, AML obligation analysis and document review involved significant manual effort. The process created friction and slowed onboarding, but the regulatory and compliance implications meant that automation could not simply replace human judgement. **Task:** I needed to define an architecture that could use AI and document intelligence to accelerate the process while maintaining appropriate compliance review, traceability and human accountability. **Action:** I designed an AI-enabled solution that combined: Automated document extraction AML obligation analysis Decision support Human-in-the-loop compliance review Workflow controls Auditability and traceability The design positioned AI as an assistance and decision-support capability rather than an uncontrolled autonomous decision-maker. Compliance oversight and human review remained part of the process, particularly for decisions requiring judgement or regulatory accountability. **Result:** The solution established an architecture for reducing manual document-processing effort and accelerating onboarding while maintaining compliance controls. It provided a practical way to introduce AI into a regulated process without removing the necessary human oversight. **Lesson:** Innovation in regulated environments succeeds when controls, traceability and human accountability are designed into the solution. The objective is not to automate everything, but to apply automation where it adds value while preserving the controls required for safe and compliant decision-making.

## 12. Architectural Decision Challenged During Customer Migration
**Interview Theme or Variation Note:** Conflict management; influencing without authority; technical leadership; decision-making under pressure; stakeholder management; design challenge; building consensus. **Also use for:** “Tell me about a time your recommendation was challenged”; “Tell me about a conflict with engineering stakeholders”; “How do you respond when implementation exposes a design issue?” **Situation:** During the NAB Customer Master migration program, I designed a migration approach that had completed its reviews and been approved. During system integration testing, the engineering team identified a race condition that challenged one of the architectural assumptions. This created a potential reliability risk, engineering managers questioned whether the design should change, and the lack of agreement placed the migration timeline at risk. **Task:** My responsibility was to assess the concern objectively, determine whether the architecture needed to change, and guide the stakeholders toward a decision that balanced reliability, delivery impact, complexity and long-term supportability. **Action:** I worked closely with the engineers to understand and validate the concurrency scenario and remained open to revisiting the original design. I helped develop several viable options and made the trade-offs across technical risk, delivery effort and supportability explicit. I then involved my manager and brought the relevant engineering managers together for a focused review. I facilitated an evidence-based discussion centred on the technical facts and migration impact rather than individual opinions, and guided the group toward an agreed implementation approach. **Result:** The stakeholders reached consensus, the race condition was addressed during SIT, and the migration proceeded without carrying the identified reliability risk into production. The transparent and collaborative process also strengthened trust between architecture and engineering. **Lesson:** Architecture should withstand challenge. My role is not to defend every original design decision, but to help the organisation reach the best outcome based on evidence, risk and clearly understood trade-offs.

## 13. Receiving Difficult Feedback — Migration Design Assumption
**Interview Theme or Variation Note:** Receiving difficult feedback; professional maturity; openness to challenge; collaboration with engineering; evidence-based decision-making; continuous learning. **Also use for:** “Tell me about difficult feedback you received”; “How do you react when someone questions your design?”; “Describe a time you changed your recommendation after receiving new evidence.” **Situation:** During the NAB Customer Master migration program, my design had completed its reviews and been approved. During SIT, the engineering team gave me difficult but valid feedback: one of the design assumptions did not fully account for a concurrency scenario, and under that scenario the migration could encounter a race condition. Their concern was that the design, although functionally sound, was not sufficiently robust for that execution path. **Task:** As the solution architect, I needed to assess the feedback objectively, determine whether the solution required a change, and protect the migration outcome without reacting defensively or allowing ownership of the original design to influence the decision. **Action:** I listened carefully and asked the engineers to walk me through the execution sequence, test evidence and potential impact. I acknowledged that implementation and SIT had exposed a scenario that had not been fully visible during the design stage. I worked with the team to validate the issue, develop alternative solutions and compare their trade-offs. Based on the evidence, I supported revising the approach and helped align the engineering managers on the required implementation. **Result:** The design was strengthened, the race condition was resolved before production, and the migration proceeded safely. The way the feedback was handled also strengthened trust between architecture and engineering because the team saw that the decision was driven by evidence rather than ego or hierarchy. **Lesson:** Good architects expect their assumptions to be tested. Receiving difficult feedback objectively, validating it carefully and adapting when the evidence supports a change produces better solutions and stronger working relationships.
